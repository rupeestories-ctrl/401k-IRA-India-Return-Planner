FATCA & CRS — Automatic data sharing between the US and India
Your financial institutions report to governments. Countries share that data.
FATCA (US)
The Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (2010) requires all US financial institutions to report account holders' balances, distributions, and activity to the IRS — including 401k and IRA withdrawals. There is no opt-out. This reporting happens automatically, before you file a return.
CRS (OECD)
India participates in international financial information exchange. Cross-border data sharing means that retirement account distributions reported to the IRS may also reach India's income tax authorities. The exact mechanism and scope of this exchange can vary, but the practical conclusion is that non-disclosure carries real risk.
What this means
The question "who will catch me if I withdraw and leave?" has a direct answer: the data is already shared. The question is not about enforcement risk — it's about what trail you leave behind and whether your return shows it correctly.

The Withholding Gap — What the IRS takes vs. what you actually owe
Mandatory withholding at source often leaves a large balance still owed at filing.

Traditional 401k 20% — mandatory No. Cannot waive. Large. At 22–32% effective rate, 20% leaves 2–12% still owed.
Traditional IRA 10% — default Yes, can elect $0 Very large if elected to $0. 10% often insufficient at income levels here.
Roth (qualified) $0 — no US tax owed N/A No US gap. India treatment unresolved (see Confidence section).
Underpayment penalty and interest

If the total you've withheld during the year doesn't cover at least 90% of your current-year tax (or 100% of prior-year tax), the IRS charges an underpayment penalty. The 2024 rate is approximately 8% annualised on the shortfall, calculated from the due date of each quarterly estimated payment. For large 401k withdrawals, this can add meaningfully to the final bill — especially if you've already left the US and missed estimated payment deadlines.

Additionally, if the balance remains unpaid after filing: failure-to-pay penalty accrues at 0.5% per month (up to 25%), plus interest at the federal short-term rate + 3% per year. On a $100K shortfall unpaid for 3 years, you could owe an additional $15–25K in penalties and interest alone.

"I'll just take the money and leave" — what actually happens
A common thought. Here's the realistic sequence of consequences.
1
You withdraw and leave
The custodian withholds at source. You receive net proceeds. You move to India. You may or may not file a final US return.
2
The IRS already has the data
Your custodian files a 1099-R. The IRS knows the full distribution amount, the tax withheld, and your SSN. This data is on file regardless of whether you file a return.
3
If you don't file: the IRS may prepare an assessment
The IRS may prepare a substitute-for-return assessment using reported 1099-R data. No deductions or credits are applied — worst-case assessment. Filing and assessment risk stays open longer when returns are missing or incomplete.
4
India's ITD sees it too
Under CRS, India's ITD receives data about your US accounts. If you haven't disclosed the foreign income on your India return (Schedule FA), you may face additional scrutiny in India as well.
5
Future US visits, income, or visa applications
An outstanding US tax liability can create problems later — especially if you have future US filing, payment, or immigration touchpoints. It can also affect US-source income flows and SSA benefits.
✓
The clean approach
File your final US return. Pay the balance with the return or set up an IRS installment plan. Request Form 4868 extension if needed. Use RNOR window to optimize — not to avoid. Leave with a clean IRS transcript.

NRA Estate Tax — the $60,000 cliff
Once you are a non-resident alien, the US estate tax exemption drops from $13.61M to just $60,000.
The cliff
US residents (citizens, GC holders) have a federal estate tax exemption of $13.61M (2024). Non-resident aliens have only $60,000. Above that, the same 18–40% marginal rates apply — on US-situs assets: US stocks, ETFs, 401k/IRA accounts, US real estate, and US bank accounts.
401k/IRA
Your 401k and IRA are US-situs assets. A beneficiary designation avoids probate but does NOT avoid estate tax. If the account is large, the estate tax on death as an NRA can be substantial. Consult a cross-border estate attorney — strategies include irrevocable life insurance trusts (ILITs) funded to cover the estate tax, or gifting US assets over time while still a US resident.
Timing
If you are GC-holder-turned-NRA and still have significant US assets, consider whether it makes sense to do a phased Roth conversion / asset drawdown before formally abandoning residency, rather than leaving a large 401k balance exposed to a 40% estate tax on death.

FDAP vs ECI — how NRA income is taxed on 1040-NR
Not all US income is taxed the same way for non-resident aliens. The type determines the rate.
Income typeCategoryTax rateForm / withholding
401k / IRA distributions ECI — deferred employer compensation Progressive 1040-NR brackets (Single, no std deduction) 1099-R; W-8BEN doesn't change rate for ECI
Dividends FDAP 30% flat (default) / 25% with W-8BEN + DTAA Art 10(2)(b) 1042-S; custodian withholds at source
Interest (US bank) FDAP Often 0% for NRA (portfolio interest exemption) 1042-S; verify with custodian
Long-term capital gains (stocks) NRA exempt if not ECI, not US real property 0% — key to cost basis reset strategy No withholding if NRA + W-8BEN filed
Rental income (US property) FDAP (or elect ECI for deductions) 30% flat gross (FDAP) / progressive net (ECI election) 1042-S; elect ECI on Form 1040-NR to deduct expenses
Key takeaway: Post-return US tax on retirement distributions depends on whether you remain a US tax resident, become NRA, and whether treaty rules apply. This planner uses a simplified path selected in assumptions. ECI treatment (progressive NRA brackets) is used in Conservative mode for temporary visa holders and those who have formally ended GC residency.
